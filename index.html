Milestone 1¶

Introduction¶

For our project, we will investigate a dataset on mice protein expression that we found on Kaggle (https://www.kaggle.com/ruslankl/mice-protein-expression). This dataset contains information on proteins that are expressed in several conditions in mice, including when mice are treated with the drug memantine (a drug used to treat Alzheimer's disease). We want to use the data to determine the effect memantine on the mice. The data contains 1080 measurements of expression per protein, for 72 mice (and each mouse had 15 measurements taken on it). The data has already been neatly categorized by the experimental conditions to which the mice were subjected and their biological traits (specifically trisomic or control gene, injected/not injected with memantine, stimulated/not stimulated to learn).

The dataset was in tidy form when we downloaded it, so not much ETL needed to be performed initially. All columns are named properly and are not variables, all empty values are set to NaN, each variable forms a row, all variables have their own column, and there is only one type of observational unit in the table with exception of five columns that are necessary for data categorization (genotype, treatment, behavior, class, and mouseID) which we set as the index for the dataframe. Otherwise, we had no challenges formatting the data since Kaggle had already done a very nice job making sure it was tidy. Downloading the data was also a very easy process and required no web scraping or tricky extraction.

Looking at the dataframe, there are 15 measurements for each mouse. For our future analysis we will most likely be taking the mean of the 15 measurements for each mouse, for each protein. There are 77 proteins expressed and 72 mice total, so we will have a total of 5544 data points on protein expression.

Milestone 2¶

Exploratory Analysis¶

As stated above, we will begin by taking the mean of the 15 measurements of protein expression for each mouse. First, however, a bit more manipulation of the data is necessary. The MouseID column contains two bits of information -- the mouse that was experimented on, and the number of the measurement that any particular row represents (i.e. 309_1 denotes the first measurement taken on mouse 309). We split this column into two columns -- one for the mouse and one for the measurement number -- so that we could then easily group by mouse and take the average of the protein expression data over the 15 trials.

Now we're going to analyze some of the proteins. A useful piece of analysis would be to find out which proteins have similar expression levels to determine if the proteins are possibly expressed through the same mechanism. If several proteins do have the same or similar expression mechanisms, looking at the differences in variables such as treated with saline or memantine could give us a clue what proteins or pathways memantine affects in the brain, or another variable such as being stimulated to learn. Let's take the protein pCREB_N and analyze the protein that has the most similar expression to it for mice stimulated to learn and either treated with memantine or saline.

An important measure to consider is whether there is consistency for the expression of a protein among all the mice in that specific class. For example, below we have plotted a bar graph showing the expression of the protein pCREB_N in mice in the c-CS-s class. The expression levels do vary a little bit for this protein in each mice. This visaul can help us decide which proteins we want to do more research on because they have more consistent expression levels in mice, meaning our conclusions will be more accurate.
